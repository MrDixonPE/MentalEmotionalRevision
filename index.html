Mental sub-factors

Mental
Mental Toughness
Tap to reveal definition
The ability to deal effectively with stress, challenge and failure and perform to your very best within a performance context. Example: A basketballer who misses a shot is determined to win the rebound and shoot again.
Mental
Anxiety
Tap to reveal definition
A feeling of worry, nervousness or unease about something with an uncertain outcome (result, opponent, crowd, weather, injury etc). Example: Tight muscles cause inaccurate passes, unforced errors in badminton, or a slow start on the whistle.
Mental
Level of Arousal
Tap to reveal definition
The preparation of the body for action. When threatened, the brain prepares the body to react (fight or flight). Coaches may psyche up or calm down athletes. Example: Awareness of arousal level allows you to control it and perform to a better level.
Mental
Decision Making
Tap to reveal definition
The ability to use our senses to identify the correct solution for a performance challenge — using sight, hearing, kinaesthetic sense, past experience, and the ability to read and predict the game. Example: Top performers make the right call at the right time using their experience of opponent, self, and environment.
Mental
Concentration
Tap to reveal definition
The ability to stay fully focused on a task for the full period of time that task is happening. Example: Allows performers to predict opponents' movements, identify space, and identify weaknesses.

Emotional sub-factors

Emotional
Confidence
Tap to reveal definition
Having belief in your own ability — willing to try challenging things and back yourself throughout. Example: Low confidence causes tension, rigid movements, and missing a smash shot in badminton.
Emotional
Anger
Tap to reveal definition
A strong feeling of annoyance, displeasure or hostility. Example: Uncontrolled anger leads to bad tackles, giving away free kicks, and handing the opposition an attacking advantage.
Emotional
Fear
Tap to reveal definition
An unpleasant emotion caused by the threat of danger or defeat. Example: Fear causes hesitation, poor technique, and avoidance of challenging situations in performance.
Emotional
Resilience
Tap to reveal definition
The ability to never give up. Maintaining a positive outlook throughout. Example: A resilient performer bounces back from mistakes and keeps striving even when behind.

Development Approaches

All three approaches can be used to develop both mental and emotional sub-factors. Click each to expand.

1. Mental Rehearsal with Music ›

The performer pictures themselves executing a skill and practises it in their mind. Music is used to help clear negativity and distraction, focusing on the upcoming performance. It aims to flush out negative thoughts and paint a picture of a quality performance with a successful outcome.

Example: Ronaldo uses mental rehearsal before matches. Some athletes use hip-hop to get hyped up; others use classical music to calm themselves down. All are different.

✓ Positives

  • No equipment needed — can be done anywhere
  • Can be used at home or in the build-up to a performance
  • Helps an injured player maintain mental sharpness
  • Allows you to rehearse dealing with challenges before they happen

✗ Negatives

  • Requires a quiet space — difficult with crowd noise
  • Must be practised regularly to feel the full benefit
  • Young athletes may struggle to keep images positive
  • Negative thoughts of past defeats can creep in if nervous
2. Positive Talk (self-talk or team talk) ›

Set phrases run over in your mind to stay calm in challenging situations. All thoughts and words must be positive and controlled.

"Low and hard to the goalkeeper's right = goal"  |  "It was a foul, just accept the referee's decision"  |  "I'm one on one with the keeper — composure"

✓ Positives

  • No equipment — can be used anywhere
  • Can be used before AND during performance
  • Versatile — can target any skill, tactic or behaviour

✗ Negatives

  • Must be practised regularly to be effective
  • Can feel self-conscious when using near others initially
3. Routines and Rituals ›

Athletes follow set routines before or during performance to maintain focus and mental order. Creates a consistent mindset going into performance.

Serena Williams bounces the ball 5 times before every serve. Nadal arranges his water bottles in a specific way — "When I do these things it means I am focused, I am competing."

✓ Positives

  • Creates a strong sense of focus and readiness
  • Can be carried out before or during performance
  • Helps block out distractions

✗ Negatives

  • Can become a distraction if the routine is disrupted
  • May not work until the habit is well established

Data Collection Methods

Mental and emotional data collection is challenging because much of it is subjective (based on opinion). It must be completed individually, prior to a performance.

SCAT Test — Sports Competition Anxiety Test ›

Used to collect data on anxiety. Completed individually, in private, before performance. Questions ask e.g. "Before I compete, I worry about not performing well" with answers: Rarely / Sometimes / Often.

Why use it? — RAVE

RReliableStandardised — same every time — allows fair comparison to the scoring system.
AAppropriate & AccurateCompleted in private; you are the best judge of your own mental state.
VValidProvides a permanent record for future comparisons to check for improvement.
EEasy to completeQuick, little equipment, instant results — training can begin straight away.
PPW — Performance Profile Wheel ›

Rate yourself on 4 mental or emotional sub-factors on a scale of 1–12 (12 = excellent, 1 = poor). Compare to a model performer to identify 1 strength and 1 development need.

Sub-factors used: Mental Toughness · Decision Making · Concentration · Anxiety · Confidence · Fear · Resilience · Anger
Quantitative vs Qualitative Data ›
Quantitative
Numbers, statistics, percentages. Objective — no personal feelings, just raw facts.Example: SCAT test score.
Qualitative
Perception and opinion. Subjective — personal opinions can affect accuracy.Example: Training diary entries.

Model Performer

A model performer can be: the world's best, a school/regional/national champion, a team mate, or a teacher/coach.

Why use one? To motivate and inspire mentally · set realistic targets · highlight outstanding mental attributes · promote performance consistency · highlight correct mindset before, during and after performance.

Goals, Monitoring & Evaluating

Every athlete should have targets they want — and can — achieve.

SMART Targets

S

Linked to your weakness and performance context
M
Measurable
You must be able to measure whether you achieved it
A
Achievable
Challenging but realistic — world #1 is unrealistic for most
R
Relevant
Must relate to the individual athlete and their activity
T
Timed
Too short = unrealistic. Too long = loses focus and motivation
Short-term goal example
By week 2 of my PDP, I will be able to carry out mental rehearsal with music effectively on my own before a football match.
Long-term goal example
By the end of my 6-week PDP, I will be able to consistently control my anxiety whilst performing in football competitions.

Monitoring & Evaluating

Monitoring (on-going)
An on-going process to track progress throughout the PDP. If improvements aren't being made, adapt the approach.
  • Method: Training diary — record how mental factors impacted each session and your next steps.
  • Easy to use, reliable, provides a written record to look back on.
Evaluating (end-point)
An end-point check — comparing performance before and after the programme to judge its effectiveness.
  • Method: Retesting — repeat the SCAT in exactly the same conditions at the end of the PDP.
  • Set protocols ensure accurate results; allows you to see clearly if the programme worked.

Feedback Types

TypeDescriptionExample
Internal (kinaesthetic)Movement awareness — feeling whether a skill was executed well without seeing the outcome.Feeling whether you struck a football sweetly before you see where it goes.

For feedback to be effective it must be: positive · precise and accurate · given as soon as possible · in bite-size chunks (too much information at once is difficult to process).

Exam Skills — Command Words

Know exactly what each command word is asking you to do and how marks are awarded.

Impact question structure — FASI

F
Factor
Name the mental or emotional sub-factor
A
Activity
Name your sport or activity
S
Situation
Describe specifically what happened in performance
I
Impact
Explain the knock-on effect on overall performance
Describe1 mark per relevant detail

State what happened in detail. Cover WHAT, WHERE, WHEN, WHO, HOW. Each relevant piece of detail earns 1 mark.

A method I used to gather data on anxiety was the SCAT test. I completed this on my own before performance (1) on the PE Cycle of Analysis app (1). I answered 10 questions (1) with options: rarely, sometimes, often (1).
Explain1 mark per paired point

Give a reason AND state the effect it has. Point (sentence 1) + Expand with effect (sentence 2) = 1 mark. They must match up.

I used positive self-talk because it required no equipment. This meant I could maximise training time and therefore improve my performance quicker. (1 mark for matching pair)
Analyse2 pros + 2 cons for 4 marks

Look at both pros AND cons. Use: Identification → Implication → Impact. Give 2 positive and 2 negative responses.

"One advantage of mental rehearsal is it requires no equipment. This means more time can be spent on actual training." | "One disadvantage is it requires a quiet space, which is difficult in a noisy changing room."
Evaluate1 mark per IJV triplet

Write in the past tense. Identify (was it effective?) → Judgement (how?) → Value (what happened to performance as a result?)

Mental rehearsal with music was effective (I) because it could be completed anywhere, keeping my game plan fresh (J). As a result, I began with confidence and played out pre-rehearsed shot sequences against my opponent (V). (1 mark)
Top tip: For Evaluate questions, always link back to your performance. You cannot be awarded the mark if you do not mention the effect on performance.
